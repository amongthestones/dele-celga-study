# Module 5: Pronouns and Clitic Placement
## Pronomes átonos e a súa colocación

---

## Overview

This is the module Spanish speakers find hardest, because Galician does two things differently:

1. It distinguishes **te** (direct object) from **che** (indirect object) in the 2nd person singular.
2. Its default position for object pronouns is **after** the verb (enclitic), not before it.

Plus the **second form of the article** (segunda forma do artigo), which is tied to verb endings. Get these right and your Galician immediately stops sounding like Spanish.

**Prerequisites:** Modules 1–4.

## Part 1: Subject Pronouns

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 1st | eu | nós |
| 2nd informal | ti | vós |
| 2nd formal | vostede | vostedes |
| 3rd masc | el | eles |
| 3rd fem | ela | elas |

There is also the colloquial/solidarity pronoun **nós/a xente**; for CELGA 2 the table above is enough.

## Part 2: Unstressed Object Pronouns (Pronomes átonos)

### Direct object (complemento directo)

| | Singular | Plural |
|--|----------|--------|
| 1st | me | nos |
| 2nd | **te** | vos |
| 3rd m | o (lo, no) | os (los, nos) |
| 3rd f | a (la, na) | as (las, nas) |

### Indirect object (complemento indirecto)

| | Singular | Plural |
|--|----------|--------|
| 1st | me | nos |
| 2nd | **che** | vos |
| 3rd | lle | lles |

> **The key Galician contrast:** in the 2nd person singular, the **direct** object is **te** and the **indirect** object is **che**.
> - *Vexo**te**.* (I see you — direct)
> - *Dou**che** un libro.* (I give you a book — indirect: "to you")
>
> A quick test: if Spanish would use *te* as "to you / for you" (indirect), Galician uses **che**. If it's the thing directly acted on, Galician uses **te**.

### The allomorphs (lo/la, no/na)

The 3rd-person forms **o/a/os/as** change shape depending on the sound before them:
- After a verb form ending in **-r or -s**, they become **-lo/-la/-los/-las** (and the -r/-s drops): *comer + o → comelo*, *colles + as → cóllelas*.
- After a form ending in a nasal/diphthong (**-n, son, fixéron...**), they become **-no/-na/-nos/-nas**: *fixeron + o → fixérono*, *colleron + as → colléronas*.

This is automatic — you'll absorb it by ear. CELGA tests that you **recognize** it.

## Part 3: Combined Pronouns (Indirect + Direct)

When two pronouns meet, indirect comes first and they often fuse:

| Indirect + o/a/os/as | Result | Example |
|----------------------|--------|---------|
| me + o | **mo** | Deumo. (He gave it to me) |
| che + o | **cho** | Doucho. (I give it to you) |
| lle + o | **llo** | Dixenllo. (I said it to him/her) |
| nos + o | **nolo** | Trouxéronnolo. (They brought it to us) |
| vos + o | **volo** | Dareivolo. (I'll give it to you-pl) |
| lles + o | **llelo** | Conteillelo. (I told it to them) |

(The same pattern holds for the feminine and plural: *ma, cha, lla; mos, chos, llos*, etc.)

## Part 4: Clitic Placement (Colocación) — the big rule

### Default: ENCLITIC (after the verb, attached)

In a plain affirmative main clause, the pronoun goes **after** the verb and is written joined to it:

- *Vin**te** onte.* (I saw you yesterday.)
- *Dixen**che** a verdade.* (I told you the truth.)
- *Levantou**se** cedo.* (He got up early.)

> This is the opposite of Spanish, which puts it before: *Te vi ayer.* Saying *"Te vin onte"* in a plain statement is a classic Spanish-interference error.

### Proclitic (before the verb) — triggered by specific elements

The pronoun moves **in front** of the verb (written separately) when one of these appears before it:

| Trigger | Example |
|---------|---------|
| **Negation** (non, nunca, nada, ninguén, tampouco) | *Non **te** vin.* · *Nunca **me** escribe.* |
| **Subordinating conjunctions** (que, se, porque, cando, aínda que…) | *Dixo que **che** gustaba.* · *Se **o** ves, dille…* |
| **Relative pronouns** (que, quen, onde…) | *O home que **me** axudou.* |
| **Certain adverbs** (xa, aínda, sempre, tamén, só, quizais, talvez) | *Xa **o** sei.* · *Tamén **che** o dixen.* |
| **Interrogatives/exclamatives with a question word** | *Onde **o** atopaches?* · *Que **lle** dixeches!* |
| **Indefinite/quantifier subjects** (alguén, todos, calquera…) | *Alguén **o** sabe.* |

**Rule of thumb:** a "plain" verb at the start of its clause → pronoun **after**. Anything that "pulls" attention before the verb (no, que, xa, question words…) → pronoun **before**.

### Infinitives, gerunds, imperatives

- **Affirmative imperative:** enclitic — *Dáme**o*** / *Cónta**llo***.
- **Negative imperative:** proclitic — *Non **mo** deas.*
- **Infinitive / gerund:** normally enclitic — *para verte*, *facéndoo* — but a preceding trigger can move it: *sen **o** ver*.

### Mesoclisis (recognition only)

With the **future and conditional**, the pronoun can sit *inside* the verb form, between stem and ending. This is a hallmark of careful Galician:
- *Dareiche* → *Darei + che* (I'll give you), and with two pronouns: *Dareicho* (I'll give it to you).
- *Faríache* (I would do for you).

You don't need to produce mesoclisis perfectly at B1, but recognize it when reading.

## Part 5: The Second Form of the Article (Segunda forma do artigo)

When the article **o / a / os / as** follows a word ending in **-r** or **-s**, that -r/-s drops and the article takes the form **lo / la / los / las**, joined with a hyphen-less spelling change:

| Before | Combined (second form) |
|--------|------------------------|
| comer + o caldo | **come-lo caldo** |
| ves + os nenos | **ve-los nenos** |
| facer + as cousas | **face-las cousas** |
| polas + os = (por + los) | already seen as **polos** |

This also happens after **us, todos, ambos, entrambos**: *todo-los días*, *entrambo-las mans*.

> **Status:** the current RAG norm treats writing the second form as **optional** — it reflects pronunciation, so *comer o caldo* and *come-lo caldo* are both acceptable in writing, but you will **hear** the second form constantly. Know it for listening and reading; in your own writing, be consistent.

This is exactly why *por + o = **polo*** and *por + os = **polos*** (Module 1): *polo* is *por* + the second-form article *lo*.

## Answer Key

### Exercise 1:
1. Véxote (direct)  2. Douche (indirect)  3. chamándote (direct)  4. Dixenche (indirect)  5. Convidáronte (direct)

### Exercise 2:
1. Non **me** gusta o café. (negation → proclitic)
2. Onte **vin**o → *Onte vino no mercado.* (plain statement → enclitic; note the allomorph after the diphthong is not triggered here, so *vino*)
3. Dixo que **che** escribiría. (subordinate → proclitic)
4. Xa **se** levantou. (adverb *xa* → proclitic)
5. Quen **o** trouxo? (question word → proclitic)

### Exercise 3:
1. Deumo.  2. Dixenllo.  3. Vou darcho. / Douche o agasallo → Doucho.

### Exercise 4:
1. come-lo pan  2. ve-las estrelas  3. todo-los días

## Common Errors

1. **Spanish-style proclisis in plain statements:** ❌ *Te vin onte* → ✓ *Vinte onte.*
2. **Using te for the indirect object:** ❌ *Doute un libro* → ✓ *Douche un libro.*
3. **Forgetting proclisis after triggers:** ❌ *Non vinte* → ✓ *Non te vin.*
