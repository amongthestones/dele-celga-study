# Module 6: Distinctively Galician Features
## Trazos propios do galego

## Overview

This module gathers the features that have **no direct Spanish equivalent** and therefore catch Spanish speakers out. None of them is hard on its own; the difficulty is remembering they exist. Each is fair game on CELGA 2.

**Prerequisites:** Modules 1–5.

---

## Part 1: Galician Has NO Compound Tenses

This is the single biggest structural difference from Spanish.

Galician does **not** use *haber + participle*. There is no *he comido, había llegado, habré terminado*. Galician expresses those meanings with **simple tenses** (sometimes with **ter + participle** as a resultative, but never as the everyday "present perfect").

| Spanish (compound) | Galician (simple) |
|--------------------|-------------------|
| He comido. | **Comín.** (I ate / I've eaten) |
| Ya he terminado. | **Xa rematei.** |
| Cuando llegué, ya había salido. | Cando cheguei, **xa saíra** (or: xa marchara). |
| Habré terminado para mañá. | Para mañá **rematarei**. |

- The everyday past is the **pretérito** (*comín, fixen, fun*).
- For "past before another past", Galician has a **synthetic pluperfect** in *-ra*: *cantara, comera, saíra, fora* ("had sung/eaten/left/been/gone"). This *-ra* form is past indicative in Galician (unlike Spanish, where *-ra* is mainly subjunctive).

> ✅ Takeaway: when you'd reach for *haber + participle* in Spanish, **don't**. Use a simple Galician tense.

## Part 2: The Personal (Inflected) Infinitive — Infinitivo conxugado

Galician (like Portuguese) has an infinitive that **agrees with its subject**. Endings:

| Person | Ending | falar |
|--------|--------|-------|
| eu | — (bare) | falar |
| ti | **-es** | falares |
| el/ela | — | falar |
| nós | **-mos** | falarmos |
| vós | **-des** | falardes |
| eles/elas | **-en** | falaren |

### When it's used

Mainly when the infinitive has **its own subject different from the main verb**, or to make the subject explicit, often after prepositions:

- *Antes de **saírmos**, apaga a luz.* (Before **we** leave, turn off the light.)
- *É difícil **chegaren** a tempo.* (It's hard for **them** to arrive on time.)
- *Ao **entrares** na sala, saúda.* (When **you** enter the room, say hello.)

> At B1, aim to **recognize** it and use the most frequent cases (after *antes de, despois de, ao, para, sen* when the subject is explicit). Don't force it where a plain infinitive is natural.

## Part 3: Demonstratives and Possessives

### Demonstratives (three degrees)

| | Masc Sg | Fem Sg | Masc Pl | Fem Pl | Neuter |
|--|---------|--------|---------|--------|--------|
| this (here) | este | esta | estes | estas | **isto** |
| that (there) | ese | esa | eses | esas | **iso** |
| that (yonder) | aquel | aquela | aqueles | aquelas | **aquilo** |

The neuter forms (*isto, iso, aquilo*) refer to ideas or unidentified things: *Que é **isto**?*

### Possessives — they normally take the article

Unlike Spanish, the Galician possessive is usually **preceded by the article**:

- *o **meu** coche* (my car) — Sp. *mi coche*
- *a **miña** casa* (my house)
- *os **teus** amigos* (your friends)
- *a **súa** nai* (his/her/their mother)

| | Masc Sg | Fem Sg | Masc Pl | Fem Pl |
|--|---------|--------|---------|--------|
| my | meu | miña | meus | miñas |
| your (ti) | teu | túa | teus | túas |
| his/her/their | seu | súa | seus | súas |
| our | noso | nosa | nosos | nosas |
| your (vós) | voso | vosa | vosos | vosas |

> Exceptions where the article drops: with close family in direct address (*miña nai*, *meu pai* are common without article) and in some set phrases. The default to learn: **article + possessive + noun**.

## Part 4: Pronunciation Features to Recognize — Gheada and Seseo

These are **dialectal pronunciations**. They are **not** part of the written standard, but you'll hear them, and CELGA listening uses real Galician voices.

- **Gheada:** the *g* (and *gu*) sound pronounced as an aspirated [h]-like sound, so *gato* sounds like "hato", *amigo* like "amiho". Common across much of western Galicia.
- **Seseo:** *c/z* pronounced as [s], so *cinco* sounds like "sinco", *fazer/facer* like "faser". Common on the western coast.

> You **write** standard (*gato, cinco, facer*) regardless of how it's pronounced. Knowing gheada and seseo exist stops them from throwing you in the listening test.

Also worth knowing for listening: Galician has **seven vowels** (open and closed *e* and *o*: *é/ê*, *ó/ô*), a distinction Spanish lacks. It's rarely tested in writing but shapes how words sound.

## Part 5: A Few More Spanish-Interference Traps

| Don't say (Spanish) | Say (Galician) |
|---------------------|----------------|
| *muy* before adjective | **moi** (*moi bo*) |
| *pero* only | *pero* / **mais** (formal) |
| *entonces* | **entón** |
| *pero* + *aunque* mixing | *aínda que* + subjunctive |
| compound *he hecho* | simple **fixen** |
| *hay que* → *hai que* | **hai que** (✓ similar but spelled with -i) |

## Common Errors

1. **Importing compound tenses:** ❌ *Hei comido* → ✓ *Comín.*
2. **Dropping the article on possessives:** ❌ *Meu coche é novo* → ✓ *O meu coche é novo.*
3. **Writing gheada/seseo:** ❌ *sinco, amiho* → ✓ *cinco, amigo* (pronunciation only).
